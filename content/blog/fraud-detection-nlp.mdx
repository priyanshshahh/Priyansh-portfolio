## The problem

Fraudulent job postings are a real harm — they harvest personal data and money from people looking for work. The challenge is that scam listings are written to look legitimate. Surface keywords aren't enough; you need to model *how* the text is written, not just *what* it says.

## Feature engineering

The core insight was to combine two complementary signals:

- **TF-IDF** captures the vocabulary and phrasing patterns of a posting.
- **VADER sentiment** captures tone — scam posts often skew unnaturally positive ("urgent!", "guaranteed income!").

<Callout title="Why hybrid features won" variant="architecture">
TF-IDF alone confuses unusual-but-legitimate postings with fraud. Sentiment alone is too coarse. Together they separate "weird wording" from "manipulative tone" — and that combination is where the accuracy came from.
</Callout>

## The model

After comparing several classifiers, the pipeline landed at **96.5% accuracy**. The winning setup balanced precision and recall so the system flags fraud aggressively without burying legitimate listings in false positives.

| Metric | Result |
|--------|--------|
| Accuracy | 96.5% |
| Features | TF-IDF + VADER |
| Interface | Real-time Dash dashboard |

## Making it usable

A model in a notebook helps no one. I wrapped the pipeline in a **real-time Dash dashboard** where a posting can be pasted in and scored instantly, with the contributing signals shown — so the decision is explainable, not a black box.

## Lessons

- **Feature design beat model choice.** The hybrid feature set moved the needle more than swapping algorithms.
- **Explainability is a feature.** Showing *why* something was flagged turned a classifier into a tool people trust.
