## The question

Large language models can summarize a 10-K, but can they make *allocation decisions* that hold up against classical quant baselines? That was the question behind my **URECA** (Undergraduate Research & Creative Activities) research paper.

## The data pipeline

Everything started with clean, point-in-time data. I built a **SQL pipeline over WRDS** (Wharton Research Data Services) to extract fundamentals and pricing without lookahead bias — the silent killer of finance research.

<Callout title="Point-in-time discipline" variant="results">
If your model "knows" a restated earnings number before it was public, your backtest is fiction. Most of the engineering effort went into making sure every feature was available at decision time — not after.
</Callout>

## The strategies

I evaluated **prompt-engineered strategies** driving two model families:

- **Gemini** — for structured reasoning over fundamentals.
- **Perplexity API** — for retrieval-grounded context on each name.

Each strategy was specified as a prompt template that turned raw financial context into a ranked set of allocation decisions, which were then backtested against the same universe.

## What I found

- **Prompt design dominates.** The gap between a naive prompt and a carefully structured one was larger than the gap between models.
- **Grounding matters.** Retrieval-augmented context reduced hallucinated reasoning about companies.
- **LLMs are strong at narrative, weak at calibration.** They rank plausibly but are overconfident — position sizing still needs a classical risk layer.

The paper's conclusion: LLMs are a genuinely useful **idea-generation and reasoning layer**, but they belong *inside* a disciplined quant pipeline, not in place of one.

## Takeaway

The most transferable skill wasn't prompt-wrangling — it was treating an LLM like any other noisy estimator: measure it honestly, bound its confidence, and never let it size the trade alone.
